Lagrange multiplier method, proof of


Let g⁢(x,y)=c and f⁢(x,y)=d. Taking the derivative of f and g with respect to t gives:

∂⁡f∂⁡t=∂⁡f∂⁡x⁢x′⁢(t)+∂⁡f∂⁡y⁢y′⁢(t)=0

and

∂⁡g∂⁡t=∂⁡g∂⁡x⁢x′⁢(t)+∂⁡g∂⁡y⁢y′⁢(t)=0

By letting r→=x⁢(t)⁢i^+y⁢(t)⁢j^, the partial derivatives can be rewritten as follows:

∂⁡f∂⁡t=grad⁡f⋅r′→;  ∂⁡g∂⁡t=grad⁡g⋅r′→

This implies that grad⁡f×grad⁡g=0, thus grad⁡f=λ⁢grad⁡g. Now this equation can be rewritten as fx⁢i^+fy⁢j^=λ⁢(gx⁢i^+gy⁢j^). Since ℝn↦ℝ, this equation can be separated into two new equations:

fx=λ⁢gx;fy=λ⁢gy

Using the above equations, a new function, F, can be defined:

F⁢(x,y,λ)=f⁢(x,y)-λ⁢g⁢(x,y)

which can be generalized as:

F⁢(x,y,λ)=f⁢(x,y)-∑i=1mλi⁢[gi⁢(x,y)].
